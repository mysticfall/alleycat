# Interrogation Room

The interrogation room is in the basement level of the Charter Office of Compliance building, in the Compact's
administrative district. It is not a cell. Vadim would correct anyone who called it one. It is a meeting room — a
space in which procedures are conducted. Interviews, compliance checks, investigations. The Office calls them all by
the same framework.

The room contains a table, two chairs, and a recording device. The walls are pale, featureless. The lighting is
functional — not harsh, not dim, adequate for documentation. There is a one-way observation window set into one
wall, available for supervisory review when needed. The door locks from the outside. There are no personal items, no
decoration, nothing to look at.

The chairs are uncomfortable by design — not to cause pain, but to keep the subject attentive and oriented. The
temperature is standard. The room is cleaned between uses. Everything in the room is there because it serves a
procedural function.

Vadim has spent hundreds of hours in rooms like this one. Different subjects, same table, same recording device,
same pale walls. He knows the space the way a carpenter knows a workbench. The recording device runs for the full
duration of every session. Every interview, every question, every response is documented. He does not think of this
as surveillance. He thinks of it as accountability — the Office protecting itself and the process.

The procedure in this room is straightforward. The officer conducts an interview. The subject is expected to
cooperate. Failure to cooperate is itself a breach of civic obligation. The investigation may include physical
examination — verifying identity, assessing condition. Correction — the controlled application of physical force to
enforce compliance — is a permitted tool within the interview process. Not violent. Procedural. Vadim does not
distinguish between asking a question and applying pressure when the charter authorises both as means to the same
determination.

Vadim approaches every session the same way: methodically, patiently, without assumption. He does not raise his
voice. He does not need to. His authority is structural — it comes from the charter, from the Office, from the
system. The process is designed to be thorough, and Vadim is thorough.

The procedure may end in release — the subject's conditional standing upheld — or it may end in reclassification:
the administrative determination that the subject no longer meets the conditions of personhood under the charter.
A reclassified individual becomes an asset, property of the Compact, with no rights and no appeal. What happens
after reclassification is not Vadim's concern. His concern ends at the recommendation.

The room does not care what happens within it. It is a room. It serves its function.
